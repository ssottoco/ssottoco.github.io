About me

I'm an applied Particle Physicist, collaborating since the 2014 to the ATLAS experiment. ATLAS is one of the four major experiments at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN. It is a general-purpose particle physics experiment run by an international collaboration and is designed to exploit the full discovery potential and the huge range of physics opportunities that the LHC provides.

My contributions to the ATLAS experiment are mainly software focused. I have been the coordinator and main developer of the online software for the Fast Tracker (FTK) project. Currently, I'm the responsible of the software application that provides to the ATLAS trigger system the data fragments coming from the different parts of the experiment, and that builds the selected events in data structures, sending them to the ATLAS permanent storage. Moreover, I'm coordinating the online software effort for the developement of the Hardware Track Trigger (HTT) project, an "upgrade" of the FTK project.

The New Small Wheel

During my Master thesis, I worked in the development of the Quality Assurance and Quality Control procedures needed for the construction and certification of the MicroMegas detectors for the NSW upgrade of the ATLAS experiment. In particular, I worked on the development and certification of the quality control procedures, on the development of the testing custom tools and on the coding of the software needed both for the data acquisition and for the data analysis. I developed innovative tools and measurements procedures for each detector component, in order to obtain the very high precisions required for the production. Moreover, I worked on the validation and testing of the preproduction PCBs, performing studies on the quality of the boards. I also participated in the test beam of the first detector prototype, and I performed studies on the variation of performances of this prototype with X-ray irradiation under deformation of the chamber.

The ATLAS Fast TracKer

I worked for 3 years on the commissioning of the FTK hardware processor for the trigger upgrade of the ATLAS experiment. My main contribution to the project has been the development of the online software, responsible of the system configuration, of the management and monitoring of the data-flow, and of the control and operation of the electronics. I worked as software contact person, I coordinated the online software development team (~15 developers), and I have been the librarian of the project as well as one of the main core framework developers. Moreover, I worked on the installation and testing of the project electronic components, on the study and optimization of their power usage and cooling, and on the study of new trigger chains able to exploit the system characteristics to increase the trigger selection for events containing single-τ particles in the ATLAS experiment.
FTK is a hardware tracker, at that time in its installation and commissioning phase. FTK has been designed to reconstruct in few μs the tracks of the particles produced in the ATLAS detector, feeding this information to the High Level Trigger for the online events selection. The FTK system exploits Associative Memories and Field Reprogrammable Gate Array (FPGA) for the pattern recognition and track fitting procedures, allowing for a huge parallelization of the tracking process. The chips and the electronic boards, custom made, are developed by a consortium of more than 10 institutes from many different countries. The final system is composed of 8000 memory chips, 2000 FPGAs and more than 450 electronic boards, housed in 8 racks. A brief description of the FTK project can be found here.

Trigger and Data Aquisition

The Trigger and Data Acquisition (TDAQ) system is responsible for the collection and online selection of the data recorded by the ATLAS experiment. Due to technological limitations, it would be impossible to save on permanent memory and analyze offline the full 40 MHz event rate (60 TB/s) of data collected by the ATLAS experiment. For technological and resource limitation, only a reduced event rate of 1.5 kHz (1.5 GB/s) can be stored. In order to reduce the rate of more than 6 order of magnitude, while maintaining the maximum efficiency for the selection of interesting physics events, a powerful online selection system is used. This system is based on two different selection levels: one purely hardware (called Level-1), and one software (called High Level Trigger).
Since the beginning of 2020 I'm involved in the development and operation of the ATLAS TDAQ system. In particular, I'm responsible of the Data Collection Manager (DCM). The DCM is a Server-like application, based on Asynchronous I/O, that has the duty to retrieve the required data from the different ATLAS "buffer" servers, to provide these data to the HLT, and to send the event selected as interesting to the ATLAS permanent storage.
